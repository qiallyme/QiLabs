Running your own business presents opportunities, but to avoid being among the 18% of small businesses that fail within their first year, you need a plan. Creating a business budget allows you to look closely at your assets, expenses, and financial goals.

What is a business budget?

A business budget is an outline of an organization’s revenue, expenses, and profit over a period of time—generally monthly, quarterly, or annually. A good business budget assigns a purpose to every dollar your business earns.

Solid planning provides a roadmap for spending and earning, creating a lens into your organization’s financial future.

Types of budgets for businesses

Master Budget

The big-picture view encompassing all finances (income, expenses, cash flow) usually for a year.

Operating Budget

Focuses on day-to-day operations, including sales, production, and marketing costs.

Cash Flow Budget

Projects cash inflows and outflows to anticipate shortfalls, vital for seasonal businesses.

Sales Budget

Focuses specifically on projected sales revenue, broken down by product or customer.

Labor Budget

Outlines labor costs including wages, benefits, and payroll taxes.

Capital Budget

Deals with long-term investments in assets like equipment, property, or technology.

Budgeting Methods

Depending on your organization, one of these methods might suit you best:

  • Incremental budgeting: Starts with your previous budget and adjusts based on anticipated changes. Simple but less flexible.
  • Zero-based budgeting: Starts from scratch. Every expense must be justified. Time-consuming but great for cutting costs.
  • Value proposition budgeting: Aligns the budget with core values and customer value.
  • Activity-based budgeting: Links the budget to specific projects or activities.

How to create a budget in 6 steps

1

Calculate all forms of income

Aggregate all revenue streams to determine how much you can afford to spend.

2

Subtract fixed expenses

Deduct costs that remain consistent, such as rent, utilities, loan payments, and salaries.

3

Subtract variable expenses

Estimate fluctuating costs like materials, commissions, and billable staff wages based on old invoices.

4

Prepare for emergency expenses

Set aside a cash reserve for unexpected equipment repairs or events.

5

Create a P&L statement

Your Profit and Loss statement serves as the baseline. It shows if you are in the black (profitable) or red (loss).

6

Draft the budget

Outline expenditures and limits for the next period (month, quarter, or year).

Customization & Best Practices

Remember that budgets are living documents. Considerations include:

  • Seasonality: Account for fluctuations in customer buying behavior.
  • E-commerce: Watch for hidden costs like shipping and platform fees.
  • Startups: Use conservative estimates if you lack historical data.

Tips for Success: Consider how changes (like hiring) impact taxes and benefits. Avoid wishful thinking—be conservative with revenue estimates. Finally, keep money organized using separate bank accounts or "envelopes."

Essential Spreadsheets to Keep

Beyond the budget, ensure you maintain:

[Image of financial statement interrelationship diagram]
  • Income Statement: Tracks revenue and expenses over a specific period.
  • Cash Flow Statement: Tracks movement of cash in and out.
  • Balance Sheet: A snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific point in time.
