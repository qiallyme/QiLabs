
# Chapter 9 — The Toll of Ghosts


In the dry, air-conditioned silence of our Phoenix apartment, a space that smelled permanently of scorched drywall and trapped, synthetic dust, I discovered that you can be a prisoner in a space as small as your own body. The apartment was a beige box, a neutral territory that Jack had claimed as his own, where every surface was a testament to his occupation. But the real walls were internal, built out of the biological residuals of my own history and the crushing expectations of the man who called himself my captain.

While Jack was out "networking" with the shadows and the hopefuls of the local music industry—building a public life out of smoke, borrowed confidence, and the red-gel stage lights of the downtown bars—I was trying to survive the structural collapse of my own marrow. I wanted a baby with a fierce, desperate, biological hunger. I wanted a tangible, breathing anchor, a physical mass of my own creation that would finally grant me a piece of the world that Jack couldn't claim as inventory. I believed, with the naive intensity of the plundered, that by becoming a mother, I would finally possess a gravity that he couldn't simply redirect or command. I wanted a person who would look at me and see a sanctuary, not a mirror or a base camp.

I didn't know then that the trauma I’d carried out of Indiana, a legacy of coal dust and erratic rage, was more than just a memory. It was a biological ghost that lived in my blood, a genetic inheritance of breakage that was already rewriting the rules of my reproduction.

My mother’s presence in my life had narrowed down to a series of erratic, vibrating transmissions from the trailer in Somerville. She was navigating what she called her "Fits"—clinical, jagged breakages of reality where the map of her mind simply dissolved, leaving her wandering through the ruins of her own house. I would hear about her through the jagged, stuttering reports from neighbors who still had my number in Phoenix: how she’d spent the entire night in the front yard, methodically rearranging the lawn furniture in the pitch dark as if she were solving a puzzle that only she could see, or how she’d stand in the kitchen for hours, perfectly still, staring at a single piece of white bread until the sun went down and the RCA factory whistle blew. 

To her, the furniture was a problem of geometry that could be solved with enough movement; to me, her "fits" were a preview of my own future. I watched her disintegrate from two thousand miles away and I felt the same hollowness starting to take root in my own chest. She wasn't just my parent; she was the ghost of who I was becoming—a woman who had been compressed until there was nothing left but the ritual.

But the real toll, the one that broke the last of my marrow, was paid in blood and bathroom tiles.

Losing Christopher, and then Danny, wasn't a "medical event" to be summarized in a thin folder or explained away with clinical terminology. It was a sensory assault. 

Christopher was first. I remember the sound of the absolute, airless silence in the ultrasound room—a heavy, pressurized quiet that felt like it was squeezing the air out of my throat, making it impossible to swallow. I remember the cold, metallic weight of the hospital bed and the smell of industrial-strength sanitizer—a sharp, chemical bouquet of bleach and ammonia—that seemed to cling to my skin for weeks afterward, no matter how many times I showered. 

The technician didn't look at me. She just moved the wand over my stomach with a mechanical, dead-eyed efficiency, her face a mask of professional distance. When the doctor finally came in to tell me it was over, I didn't cry. I didn't scream. I had been trained since I was five in the discipline of the "good girl," the "invisible girl" who doesn't make a scene and doesn't attract the attention of the monster. I sat on the paper-covered table, the paper crinkling with a sound like dry leaves, and I listened to him talk about "statistics" and "nature’s way" while I felt like a vessel that had been shattered and glued back together with sand. I was a failure of the base camp.

And then there was the bathroom.

One night, the world simply fell out of me. It wasn't the "beautiful, soft-lit tragedy" you see in the movies with gentle music and a doctor’s comforting hand. It was a visceral, terrifying, biological horror. I remember sitting on the cold porcelain of the toilet in the dark, the only light the blue-grey glow of the desert moon through the narrow window. My body was vibrating with a pain that felt like it was trying to turn me inside out, a rhythmic, mechanical cramping that reached into the very center of my existence. Something fell out of me. 

I looked down, and in the harsh, flickering light of the bathroom, I saw the physical record of my failure. It looked like a small, translucent baby dinosaur—a fragile, unfinished thing with dark spots for eyes and a body that had reached the absolute limit of its capacity in a space that didn't know how to protect it. It was a piece of the graveyard I was secretly building. I didn't flush. I couldn't. I sat there for an hour, the cold tile floor biting into my feet, staring at the evidence of my own inadequacy. I felt like a plundered building, an empty space where a life was supposed to be.

Jack’s reaction wasn't grief; it was an irritation that bordered on disgust. He looked at my losses not as a personal tragedy, but as a malfunction of the "base camp equipment." He saw the blood and the tears as a disruption of his vision for the team.

"We’ll try again, Lisa," he’d say, his voice flat, mechanical, and entirely devoid of the "Simple Man" soul I had once believed he possessed. He would stand in the doorway, his hands on his hips, his premature gray hair looking like a weapon in the light. "A man needs an heir. I’m building a legacy here, and you’re just too fragile. You need to take better care of yourself. You need to stop being so goddamn weak."

He didn't hold me while I bled. He didn't ask about the name I had already chosen in my head—a name I kept locked in the dark room of my spirit. He used my grief as a tactical tool for further control, framing every miscarriage as a personal failure of my "partnership" duties. I was made to feel like I was the one who had discarded the life inside me, just as I had been discarded on that 3:00 AM walk in Indiana. I was the "Invisible Girl" who couldn't even manage to make a visible child. I was a machine that was eating its own product.

By the time Jack came home with the red pen and the newspaper ad for the *Busybody*, I was already hollow. I had been a "wife" for two years, and I had learned the hard, metallic lesson that in the geometry of Jack’s world, I was only as valuable as the things I could produce. If I couldn't produce an heir, I was just a liability with a pretty face—a piece of inventory that was beginning to depreciate in value.

The ad was circled in red, a violent, urgent mark on the page.

"They're looking for dancers, Lisa," he said, his voice smooth again, his "musician" smile back in place. "The pay is good. Better than anything you’ll find at the supermarket. It’s an opportunity for us. A way to get some real cash in the bank for the next session."

I looked at the ad, and then I looked at the bathroom door.

On the stage, I realized, I wouldn't have to be a mother. I wouldn't have to be a wife. I wouldn't have to be a vessel for ghosts or a graveyard for baby dinosaurs. In the heavy pink gels and the amber filters of the club, I wouldn't have to worry about my mother’s "fits" or the sound of things hitting the bathroom floor in the dark. I could just be a surface. I could be the mirror I had been trained to be since I was five, reflecting the light and the hunger so that no one could see the hollow architecture I carried inside me. I could trade my marrow for the light, and for the first time in my life, I wouldn't have to pray for the dark. 

I took the paper from his hand. The ink was cold.

"I’ll do it," I said.

And I meant it. I was ready to leave the base camp. I was ready to step out of the silence of the apartment and into the noise of the club, where the only thing that mattered was the weight of the tips and the height of the heels. I was eighteen years old, I was a graveyard, and I was finally ready to dance.

The first time I walked into the *Busybody*, the smell hit me like a memory of Somerville—the bleach, the cigarettes, the smell of people trying to be something they weren't. I felt a sudden, sharp surge of familiarity. This was a factory, too. Just like the RCA plant, just like the coal mines, this was a place where people traded pieces of themselves for a residuals check. 

But here, I was the one holding the pole. Here, the bouncers were the ones who managed the "climate of fear," not me. I watched the other girls, the way they moved, the way they used the light as a shield, and I felt a sense of relief that was colder and harder than anything I'd felt since the ultrasound. I didn't need a savior, and I didn't need a captain. I needed a coordinate. 

I sat at the vanity and looked at my face. I saw the hollows under my cheekbones, the shadows where the ghosts of Christopher and Danny lived. I picked up the heavy crimson lipstick and I painted them away. I turned my eyes into mirrors, my skin into a reflectant surface. I was no longer the girl who bled on the bathroom floor. I was the product.

Jack liked the cash. He liked the "Upfront Rule." He liked the way the other musicians looked at me when he brought me to the bars after my shift. He didn't see the graveyard anymore; he only saw the trophy. And as long as the inventory was high, he was satisfied.

I spent my nights in the pink gels, my shins vibrating with the bass, my teeth rattling with the music. I was a master of the "Mirror Effect," giving the men in the dark exactly what they wanted to see while I stayed locked in that dark, interior room. I was the "Visible Girl" now, the one whose every gesture was a transaction. 

But sometimes, in the absolute, air-conditioned middle of the night, I would wake up and feel the silence of the ultrasound room again. I would feel the cold metallic weight of the hospital bed. I would think about the baby dinosaur on the bathroom floor and I would wonder if I was ever going to be more than a surface. I would look at Jack, sleeping beside me, his premature gray hair a reminder of the hook that was still set in my jaw, and I would feel a sense of thirst that no amount of Arizona water could ever quench. 

I was nineteen years old, I was a stripper, and I was the Madam of my own internal ghosts. I was waiting for the moment when the gravity of the stage would finally be stronger than the weight of the hand. I was waiting for the moment when the "Invisible Girl" would finally have enough mass to break the floorboards. 

The view from the pole was getting clearer every night. I could see the exits. I could see the technicality of the escape. And I knew that when the time came, I wouldn't be looking for a savior. I would be looking for the scissors.
