
# Prologue — The Mirror Effect


The lights in the Busybody were never just lights. They were heavy, saturated pink gels and warm amber filters, calibrated with a clinical precision by people who understood that desire is a fragile, conditional thing—a glass ornament easily shattered by the sight of a stray bruise, a stretch mark, or the sharp, protruding angles of a hunger that couldn't be satisfied by a lap dance. The pink glow made the air in the club feel thick and viscous, as if you were moving through a warm, humid fluid rather than oxygen. It tasted of stale Michelob, the metallic, ozone bite of high-tar cigarettes, and the sharp, clinical, nose-stinging smell of the bleach the porters used to mop the stage between sets. It was a chemical bouquet that worked its way into the pores of your skin and stayed at the back of your throat for hours after you’d driven home and scrubbed your face clean.

I stood in the wings on a Tuesday night in 1994, the heavy velvet curtain a rough, dusty weight against my shoulder. Behind me, the dressing room was a storm of hairspray and nervous, caffeinated energy. The heat from the ring of exposed incandescent bulbs around the vanities was a physical presence, raising the temperature in the room ten degrees higher than the rest of the club. It smelled of burnt hair from overworked curling irons, the heavy, powdery scent of expensive foundation, and the raw, salt smell of dozens of women sweating under synthetic lace and silk. We were a factory of fantasy, a high-functioning assembly line where the product was distraction. We didn't talk much; we just worked the brushes, snapped the gum, and checked the vertical line of our heels with a focused, professional detachment.

The bass of the house track—something heavy, electronic, and late-night—rattled the floorboards beneath my feet with a sound like a distant landslide. The vibration traveled up through the four-inch clear plastic heels of my shoes, vibrating in the marrow of my shins before settling in the back of my teeth. I could feel the crowd out there in the dark, a low-frequency hum of expectation and biology. I didn't see them as men anymore; I saw them as a shifting mass of eyes and potential revenue, a ledger of opportunities that needed to be managed. They weren't waiting for a person named Lisa. They were waiting for a product I had spent ten long years perfecting—the Madam, the Star, the woman who could reflect any hunger they brought with them.

I turned back to my vanity and leaned into the mirror, my hands gripping the edge of the laminate. The lighting here was different—a harsh, flickering fluorescent strip that hummed in a low, agonizing key, a sound like a trapped insect. It was the kind of light that took everything and gave nothing back, stripped of the amber filters that kept the illusions intact.

For the first time in a decade, I didn’t see the performer. I didn’t see the "Madam" with her calculated smiles and her absolute control over the room.

I saw the ghost.

I was twenty-nine years old, but in this unflinching light, the geometry of my face looked ancient, like a map of a country that had been colonized by too many wars. The meth had carved new, deep, skeletal shadows under my cheekbones, creating a hollow architecture that no amount of contouring or highlight could fix. My skin felt like parchment pulled too tight over a frame that was beginning to crack. My eyes were glassy, the pupils blown wide and fixed, staring back at a woman I hadn't really spoken to since the day I ran from the Monster in the Arizona desert. I looked at the dark circles under my eyes and I realized they weren't just signs of fatigue; they were the edges of the void.

I looked down at my hands. They were resting on the laminate surface of the vanity, and they were trembling with a fine, chaotic vibration that originated deep in my nervous system. It wasn't the shakes of a beginner or the tremor of simple exhaustion; it was the sound of a machine that had been running on high-octane panic and artificial fuel for far too long. The valves were starting to bend. The engine was starting to eat itself. My body was a biological factory that had finally run out of raw materials.

I reached down and touched the three Motorola pagers clipped to my silver belt. They were heavy, constant reminders of the logistics I managed every hour of every day. I wasn't just a dancer anymore; I was the pivot point of the entire operation. I ran the floor. I managed the "Upfront Rule" with a cold, mechanical precision that made the bouncers respect me and the girls fear me. I had a mental rolodex of every man’s particular preference, every girl’s mounting debts, and every bouncer’s specific price for loyalty. I had built a fortress out of desire and commerce, a system designed to insulate me from the very world I was selling. I sat at the very top of it, the Invisible Hand, deciding who sat where and exactly how much it would cost them to feel powerful for an hour.

But in the reflection of that defaced mirror, the fortress looked like a cage. The pagers felt like weights designed to hold me under the water. I realized that the "Madam" was just another disguise, a more sophisticated version of the "Invisible Girl" from Somerville. I was still hiding; I just had better lighting and a higher percentage of the take.

I leaned closer until my breath fogged the glass, a small, vanishing cloud of evidence that there was still a living organism behind the makeup.

"Lisa," I whispered. My own name sounded like a foreign language, a word from a country I hadn't visited in years, a dialect that had been replaced by the numerical codes of the pager.

The woman in the glass didn't blink. Her expression was flat, a terrifying, absolute emptiness that suggested she had already left the building and simply forgotten to tell me. She was a residual, a byproduct of a decade spent as a mirror for other people’s needs.

The music out on the floor shifted—a new track, the beat faster, more insistent, more predatory. I knew that the music was going to stop soon. Not because the DJ would cut the power or because the police would raid the club, but because the cost of the performance had finally exceeded the profit. I was trading in a currency I couldn't afford to lose anymore—the very marrow of my own spirit. I was being hollowed out by the very world I claimed to control, consumed by the fantasy I was selling to men who didn't even know my name.

I picked up a white tissue and wiped the heavy, crimson lipstick from my mouth. I did it with a single, violent, horizontal motion that left a red smear across my cheek like a fresh cut, a mark of breakage. I didn't fix it. I didn't lean back in to check the symmetry of my eyeliner. I didn't reach for the hairspray.

I turned my back on the mirror, leaving the Madam trapped in the fluorescent glare with her pagers, her secrets, and her hollow architecture. I walked past the other girls, who were still SNAP-SNAP-SNAPPING their gum and checking their lace, past the heavy velvet curtains that led to the stage and the pink gels. I headed for the heavy steel door at the back of the club, the one that led to the alley. 

It was late, and the Indiana night was thick with humidity. The alley was filled with the smell of wet asphalt, dumpster rot, and the distant, familiar exhaust of a factory shift change. But when I pushed that door open and stepped out of the neon pulse, the dark felt like the first honest thing I had touched in years. I stood in the puddles, the cold water seeping into my clear plastic heels, and I took a breath of air that didn't taste like Michelob or bleach. I was twenty-nine years old, I was a Madam, and I was finally starting to look for the exit.

The alleyway was a canyon of shadows, the only light coming from a single, buzzing sodium lamp that cast a sickly orange glow over the brick walls. I felt the weight of the pagers on my hip again—one for the escort service, one for the club, one for the personal calls I never wanted to answer. They were like anchors. I reached down and unclipped the first one, the Motorola that managed the girls' schedules. I held it for a moment, feeling the cold plastic, then I threw it into the open dumpster. It hit the metal with a dull, final *thud*.

I unclipped the second pager, the one for the club. I dropped it into the wet asphalt without looking back.

The third one I kept, for now. 

I started walking away from the neon sign of the Busybody, my clear heels clicking against the pavement with a sound like a countdown. I didn't have a plan. I didn't have a map. I only had the sensory memory of the girl who had dug graves in the coal dust and the woman who had survived the Monster. I was walking toward a future that didn't have a price tag, a world where the only light that mattered was the one I produced myself.

As I reached the street, I saw a car go past—a late-model sedan, its headlights cutting through the fog like searchlights. It looked like every car I had ever sat in, every car that had promised a sanctuary and delivered a transaction. I didn't look at the driver. I didn't wait for him to stop. I just kept walking, my head up, my eyes fixed on the distant, flickering lights of the horizon. 

I was Lisa Annette. I was a survivor of the "Business of Pleasure." And for the first time in my life, I wasn't a mirror. I was the star of a story that was finally mine to tell. 

The pink gels were behind me now, nothing but a glow on the clouds. I turned the corner and disappeared into the dark, a ghost who was finally learning how to be seen. 

I knew that tomorrow would bring the fallout. Jaye would be looking for his percentage. The girls would be looking for their schedules. The bouncers would be looking for their shifts. The "Madam" would be a legend, a story whispered in the dressing rooms, a woman who walked out and left the lights on. But I didn't care about the fallout. I was looking for the marrow. I was looking for the girl who had color-coded a horse purple because it was the only power she had.

I walked until the sound of the club was a memory, until the smell of the bleach was gone from my throat. I stood on a bridge over a small, stagnant river, looking down at the reflection of the streetlights in the dark water. I saw myself—not the ghost, not the Madam, but the woman. And for the first time in my thirty years, I didn't flinch.

The "Mirror Effect" was broken. I was the only light in the room, and the view from the bridge was exactly what I needed it to be. 

I took a deep breath of the damp Indiana air, feeling it fill my lungs without the taste of ozone or tobacco. I was free. And it felt exactly like the first step on a stage that didn't have a pole. It felt like coming home.
