
# Chapter 4 — Bill


I didn't fall in love with Bill. In the raw, atmospheric pressure of 1979, love was a luxury I didn't have the metabolic energy to maintain. I fell in love with the structural integrity of his car, a machine that functioned as the only boundary I could successfully close against the world.

It was a 1968 Chevy Impala—long, heavy, and blue, a shade of metallic azure that had been dulled by years of Indiana road salt and industrial fallout from the RCA plant. The white vinyl top was a landscape of decay, beginning to peel at the edges in jagged, brittle flakes that looked like a sunburnt shoulder. When I ran my fingers along the roof, the material would crumble into a fine, white powder that stayed under my fingernails for days. Inside, the car was a sensory sanctuary. It smelled of stale Camel filters, the kind that had been crushed into the overflowing chrome ashtray until the ash was a grey, compact silt. It smelled of a cheap, cloying musk perfume Bill bought at the Rexall, and the faint, sweet, persistent scent of 10W-30 motor oil that seemed to emanate from his very pores. 

But the most important thing about that car, the thing that made it a holy place to me, was the heater. The fan didn't just turn; it roared with a mechanical, prehistoric ferocity, vibrating the entire dashboard and producing a dry, artificial heat that was the most valuable commodity in the winter of 1979. It was a heat so intense it made the air inside the cabin shimmer, a steel bubble of autonomy where the biting Indiana wind couldn't reach me. When those heavy doors clicked shut—a solid, metallic *thud* that felt as final as a bank vault—the world of Somerville became a silent, grainy film playing through the safety of the glass. The shouting of my father and the suffocating, broom-heavy silence of my mother were reduced to distant, meaningless static.

Bill was eighteen when I was thirteen. In a town with a future, that would have been a crime recorded in manila folders, discussed in hushed tones in courtrooms, and resulted in a permanent brand of shame. In Somerville, it was just another Tuesday night. I was a girl from "that" family—the one in the battered, sagging trailer with the crazy mother who hacked her hair off in the kitchen and the drunk father who threw salt shakers at the appliances. People looked at me and saw a residual, a byproduct of a failing system. Bill was just a kid with a job at the gas station and a car that ran. The local police didn't look twice at us when we cruised Main Street. They had their own barometers to read, their own domestic fires to put out, and as long as we weren't breaking windows or stealing copper wire, we were invisible to the law.

He was simple in a way that felt like a vacation from the complexity of my own house. He worked at the gas station on the corner of Main and Vine, his hands permanently etched with the black grease of other people’s machines, the oil worked so deep into the cracks of his knuckles that it looked like a tattoo. His fingernails were rimmed with a yellow stain from his heavy tobacco habit, and he always carried the sharp, acidic scent of gasoline on his clothes. He had a laugh that was flat, honest, and entirely devoid of the sharp, hidden hooks that defined the men in my life. When Claude laughed, you looked for an exit; when Bill laughed, he was just happy to be sitting in a warm car. When he looked at me, he didn't see a "ghost" or an "invisible girl" or a vessel for his own rage. He saw a pretty thing that he could display in the passenger seat of his Impala, a blonde accessory that gave him a sense of status he didn't have at the gas station.

And I let him, because the alternative was a proximity I could no longer endure.

The failure of protection in our home was absolute, a vacuum where safety should have been. My brother, Tony, had been creeping into my room since I was five years old, a rhythmic, terrifying invasion of my physical space that had started at my grandmother’s house and followed us like a parasitic shadow to every new trailer we occupied. It was a slow, mechanical process, the sound of the door latch clicking, the weight of the mattress shifting, the smell of his unwashed skin filling the small room. I had learned early that telling didn't work. The language of truth was a dialect no one in my house spoke. I had seen him with my mother; I had seen him with my sister. The silence in our home wasn't just an absence of words; it was a conspiracy of survival, a collective agreement to look away so the structure wouldn't collapse completely. If my mother couldn't protect her own hair from the scissors, she certainly wasn't going to protect me from the boy in the next room. The law was a distant, abstract concept that only appeared when my father drew a knife on a cop in the front yard.

So, I looked for a different kind of wall. I looked for Bill and his blue Impala.

We would park down by the limestone quarry, the gravel crunching under the heavy tires with a sound like breaking bone. He would turn off the engine to save gas, but he’d leave the electronics on low, the battery draining as the radio played Led Zeppelin’s *Kashmir* or Bad Company’s *Feel Like Makin' Love*—the heavy-rotation soundtrack of boys who believed their youth was a permanent, unbreakable condition. The lights from the RCA plant and the distant flares of the quarry heights would reflect off the black, stagnant water of the pit, and for a few hours, I could pretend I was a passenger in a different life.

The car was always dark, lit only by the green, radioactive-looking glow of the radio dial and the intermittent orange flare of Bill’s cigarettes. Every time he took a drag, his face would be momentarily illuminated—the sharp line of his jaw, the vacant, simple focus in his eyes. He’d lean back, his arm draped over the back of the white vinyl seat, his oil-stained fingers playing with the split ends of my hair.

"You're special, Lisa," he would say, his voice a low, vibrating murmur that I felt in the upholstery. "You're not like the other girls in this town. They’re all just waiting to get married and move into the next trailer over. You got something... deeper. Something I can't quite put a finger on."

It was a line, of course. It was a script written by a thousand bored boys in a thousand rusted cars across the Midwest. I knew it was a lie, a tool used to loosen the tension in my shoulders, but I didn't care about the truth of the words. I cared about the utility of them. Every compliment, no matter how hollow, was a brick I could use to reinforce the fortifications of my own value. If Bill told me I was the sun, then maybe the cold shadow of Tony wouldn't be able to reach me when I went home. I was building a different kind of "residue," a layer of projected worth that I hoped would act as a shield.

But I learned quickly that there was a trade. That was the raw, unblinking economy of the backseat. If I wanted the warmth of the heater, if I wanted the protection of the blue steel, if I wanted to feel like I occupied a physical space that was mine to negotiate rather than a territory to be invaded, I had to give something back. The world didn't offer anything for free to girls from Somerville.

It wasn't a violent taking. Bill didn't have to use force; he was too simple for that. He just used the slow, inevitable erosion of boundaries that happens in a confined space. It started with a hand on a leg that lingered a second too long, the warmth of his palm seeping through the thin fabric of my jeans. Then it was a shift in proximity, a lean that left me pressed against the cold door handle with no room to move. It was a request that was phrased like a favor, a "don't you like me?" that was actually a demand for payment.

I didn't say no. I didn't want to say no. In the cold, practical atmosphere of the car, saying no meant the heater would go cold. It meant the car door would open, and I would have to walk back through the frozen grass, past the piles of coal dust, and into the silence of my mother and the proximity of my brother. Saying yes meant I had currency. It meant I had something the adult world wanted, something that gave me a seat at the table of transactions. For the first time in my life, I didn't feel like a byproduct; I felt like the owner of a resource.

I remember staring up at the perforations in the white vinyl of the car roof, counting them one by one in the dim green light while his breath came in ragged, shallow bursts against the hollow of my neck. I wasn't thinking about passion. I wasn't thinking about love or romance or the stories I read in the magazines at the Rexall. Those were stories for people who had a safety net, for girls whose fathers worked at the bank and whose brothers played football. 

I was thinking about the physics of it. I was thinking about how much weight I could bear before the structure buckled. I was thinking about gravity—the way he pulled at me, and the way I used his pull to stay anchored away from the trailer.

I was practicing. I was learning the choreography of desire—how to move, how to breathe, how to be the specific thing that someone else wanted so badly they would pay for it with their attention, their resources, and their protection. I thought I was controlling the transaction, trading pieces of my physical autonomy for a roadmap out of Somerville. I didn't realize yet that the roadmap was a recursive loop. I didn't realize that getting into Bill's car was just the first step in a very long journey where I would be the passenger for far, far too long, and that the men I chose would always have hands that smelled of motor oil and coal smoke.

Bill was my first real lesson in the business of being a woman in a town built on residuals. He taught me that I could be desired, and that desire was a tool I could use to navigate the geography of fear. But he also taught me that desire is a heavy, suffocating weight to carry when you’re fourteen years old and trying to outrun the monsters who already live in your hallway. I learned that the body is a ledger, and every time I let him touch me to stay warm, I was recording a debt that I would eventually have to find a way to pay off.

The quarry was silent that night, the water as still and dark as oil. I looked at my reflection in the side mirror, a distorted, silvered image of a girl who was already starting to disappear into the role she was playing. I wasn't Lisa Annette anymore; I was the Passenger. I was the Girl in the Impala. I was the one who survived by making herself into a commodity. And as we drove back toward the trailer park, the blue Impala cutting through the Indiana fog, I knew that the "Invisible Girl" had found a brand new way to stay hidden—right in plain sight, in the front seat of a 1968 Chevy.

The transition from the car to the house was always a physical shock. The moment I opened the door of the Impala, the dry, artificial heat would evaporate, replaced by the damp, metallic Indiana cold. The gravel of the driveway would crunch under my feet, the sound echoing off the thin walls of the neighboring trailers. I would walk toward our door, my heart performing that familiar, high-alert thud as I gauged the light in the kitchen window. If it was dim, Claude was asleep. If it was bright, the storm was still brewing.

I would slide through the door, my movements as silent as a draft of air. I’d pass my mother’s room, hearing the rhythmic, mechanical sound of her breathing, or perhaps the sniffle of a ghost. I’d pass Tony’s door, which was always cracked just a half-inch, a dark eye watching the hallway. I would reach my room, close the door, and sit on the edge of the bed in the dark.

The smell of Bill would stay on me—the Camel filters and the musk—and I would hold onto it like a talisman. I’d sit there until my toes stopped throbbing from the too-small shoes I still wore, and I’d think about the quarry. I would imagine the black water swallowing the trailer, the RCA plant, and every coal mine in the county. I would imagine myself floating in that water, invisible and untouchable, a girl who didn't need a heater or a car to stay warm.

I was fourteen, and I was already an expert in the economy of the soul. I knew that Bill wasn't my savior, but he was a temporary shelter, and in Somerville, a temporary shelter was the closest thing to a miracle we were allowed to expect. I would close my eyes and wait for the morning, the green glow of the radio dial still burned into my retinas, a tiny, glowing map of an escape I hadn't quite figured out how to complete.

As I lay there, I could hear the distant, low-frequency rumble of a coal train. It vibrated through the floorboards, through the bedframe, and into my very skin—a constant reminder that the ground was hollow and the residuals were always settling. But I wasn't afraid of the subsidence anymore. I had a car. I had a currency. I had a plan. I didn't know then that the plan would lead me to a stage, a pager, and a knife, but as I drifted off to sleep, the smell of motor oil still on my hands, I knew one thing for certain: the "Invisible Girl" was done waiting for the ground to reclaim her. She was going to be the one who chose the destination.

The next morning, the winter sun was a pale, weak disk in the grey sky, offering no warmth to the frozen soybean fields. I walked to the school bus stop, my breath blooming in white clouds. I saw Bill’s Impala drive past on Main Street, the blue paint looking almost black in the early light. He didn't wave, and I didn't look up. We both knew the transaction for the night was over, and the ledger was closed until the next Tuesday. 

I climbed onto the bus, taking a seat in the back. I looked out at the town of Somerville—the hardware store, the diner, the RCA plant—and I realized it all looked like a set from a movie I had already seen too many times. I was moving through it, but I wasn't part of it. I was the Passenger, the Student of Gravity, the Girl with the Ledger. And as the bus pulled away, leaving a plume of grey exhaust in the cold Indiana air, I felt a sense of power that was sharper and more dangerous than anything my father had ever shown me. I was fourteen years old, and I was already the architect of my own disappearance. I just needed to find a car that could take me far enough.
