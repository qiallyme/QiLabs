
# Chapter 6 — Early Pregnancy


The nausea wasn’t a morning occurrence; it was a permanent, atmospheric condition that dictated the rhythm of every hour I spent in the world. It came in slow, rolling tides that hit me with a visceral, stomach-flipping intensity during pre-algebra, the sound of the teacher’s chalk clicking against the blackboard becoming a rhythmic, agonizing percussion. It hit me in the humid, cabbage-smelling chaos of the school cafeteria, where the clatter of plastic trays and the high-pitched screams of other girls felt like a physical assault. And it hit me most hardest in the absolute, vibrating silence of the 3:00 AM trailer, when the only sound was the compressor of the refrigerator and the ragged, wet snoring of my father sawing through the thin plywood walls.

It tasted of copper and cold grease—the metallic, acidic flavor of high-voltage panic that seemed to stay in the back of my throat no matter how many times I brushed my teeth. It was the physical record of my childhood ending before I had even figured out the rules of it. I was fourteen years old, a skinny girl who still looked like a child in the dim light of the hallway, but I felt a thousand years old. I had read the clinical, detached descriptions in the health textbooks and heard the sharp, cruel, whispered jokes in the locker rooms, but understanding the biological machinery of reproduction is not the same as feeling that machinery start to turn inside your own ribs. It felt like I was being reorganized from the marrow out, my body no longer my own property but a vessel for a future I hadn't designed.

The preparation for the confirmation was a series of calculated risks. I bought the test at a drugstore three towns over, a place on the edge of a county line where the dust from the gravel pits felt different and where no one would recognize the girl in the oversized denim jacket. I had pulled the jacket’s collar up to my ears and kept my eyes fixed on the floor, my heart performing a frantic, mechanical thud against my ribs. I paid for the little white box with crumpled, grease-stained dollar bills I had systematically lifted from my father's change jar over the course of a week. Each theft was a gamble against his next explosion; I would wait until he was dead to the world in a vodka fog, then slide my small fingers into the jar, feeling for the paper among the cold, metallic coins. 

I took the test in a gas station bathroom off Highway 57, a cramped, windowless box that smelled of industrial-strength pine cleaner layered over twenty years of unwashed grease and woodsmoke. The fluorescent light overhead flickered with a rhythmic, high-frequency hum that seemed to match the vibration in my own teeth. The mirror was a silver-edged map of phone numbers, crude drawings, and desperate confessions scratched into the glass with keys and pocketknives. I sat on the cold, cracked tile floor, my back pressed against the metal door, the aluminum freezing even through my jacket. I held the plastic wand in my hand like it was a live wire. 

I sat there for five minutes, listening to the sound of the trucks roaring past on the highway, their weight shaking the very foundation of the small building. I watched the dust motes dancing in the sickly blue light.

Two lines.

They were pink—a soft, unapologetic, almost cheerful color that felt like a personal insult against the grey-black reality of my life. They were small, distinct, and irreversible. They were the mechanical end of the Invisible Girl.

I stared at that plastic wand until the edges of my vision began to blur and the room seemed to tilt on its axis. I waited for the stereotypical response I’d seen in movies—for the sudden, cleansing break of tears, for the hysterical sobbing, for the part of me that was still a child to scream out for a mother who could actually provide a sanctuary, or a father who could offer protection instead of a threat. But the scream didn't come. My nervous system had already been tempered by years of Somerville survival, forged in the furnace of the trailer and the cold isolation of the backyard dirt. Instead of panic, a strange, crystalline clarity settled over me, a feeling like a high-tension wire being drawn perfectly straight.

I looked down at my hands. They were the same small, calloused hands that had dug ceremonial graves for pill bugs in the coal dust. They were the same hands that had gripped the rusted tire iron in the backseat of Bill’s Impala. I looked at my reflection in the defaced mirror, looking past the scratched-in phone numbers and the grime. I had the same face, the same guarded, pale eyes that were too good at reading a room. But the girl who had walked into that bathroom twenty minutes ago had evaporated. She was a ghost now, a resident of a past I could no longer reach. In her place was a vessel—a structural necessity for someone else’s existence.

I walked out of the gas station and into the bright, blinding, indifferent glare of an Indiana afternoon. The world looked different—not softer, but sharper, every edge defined by the potential for collision. The cars passing on the highway weren't just vehicles anymore; they were kinetic threats I had to gauge. The cracked sidewalk beneath my feet wasn't just concrete; it was a path I had to navigate with a new, heavier center of gravity, a physical responsibility that I felt in the base of my spine. Everything was suddenly a hazard.

I didn't tell Bill. I didn't want to see the simple, honest pride in his eyes turn into a frantic calculation of escape. I didn't want to see him realize that the "Backseat Economy" had just produced a debt he wasn't prepared to pay. I didn't tell my mother, who was currently occupied with the frantic, terminal work of cutting herself out of her own life with a pair of sewing scissors. And I certainly didn't tell my father, whose rage would have burned this new, fragile possibility to ash before it even had a heartbeat. 

For a week, I carried the secret like a hot stone in my pocket, a weight that I could never put down. I went to school. I sat in pre-algebra and watched the chalk dust fall like snow. I turned in my homework on time. I sat at the Formica kitchen table and ate my dinner in the same suffocating silence where the salt shaker had once exploded, listening to the mechanical gurgle of the coffee pot and the low rumble of the factory whistle. I was practicing the final version of my camouflage, the ultimate performance of the Invisible Girl even as I was physically becoming two.

But the architecture of my life had shifted on its subterranean foundations. The silence in the house wasn't a tactical shield anymore; it was a waiting room. I realized then that my plan to eventually disappear—to simply ghost out of Somerville and find a stage where I could be a star—was a fundamental failure of logic. You cannot be invisible when you are physically taking up space for two. You cannot be a draft in a room when you are a mass that needs to be fed, sheltered, and protected. 

The world was going to see me now. It was going to judge me with the same cold, coal-dust indifference of the RCA plant. It was going to categorize me as a statistic, another girl from a broken trailer who didn't know how to keep her knees together. It was going to try to break me under the weight of a shame I didn't actually feel.

I sat on the edge of my bed that night, the wood-paneled wall cold against my back. I put my hand on my flat, boyish stomach. I didn't feel a rush of maternal love or a sense of spiritual destiny. I didn't feel the "miracle" the Sunday school teachers talked about. I felt a grim, steely determination that bordered on the mechanical. I felt like I was arming myself for a war that had been ongoing since before I was born.

*You're on your own,* I whispered to the nothingness inside me. *And so am I.*

It was the loneliest promise I had ever made, and the first one I intended to keep at any cost.

Living with the secret was like carrying a persistent, low-frequency vibration in my bones. Every time I looked at my mother, I wondered if she had felt this same weight, this same sudden retraction of the future. I watched her move through the kitchen, her movements jerky and puppet-like, and I wondered if she had ever made a promise like mine, or if she had simply let the tide pull her under until only the headscarf and the broom were left. I saw the way she looked at my father—a gaze of absolute, exhausted compliance—and I knew that I would rather bleed out on Highway 57 than ever allow myself to be managed that way.

The pregnancy made the trailer feel even smaller, the walls more precarious. The brown paneling seemed to be closing in, the smell of grease and tobacco becoming a personal, offensive intrusion. I spent as much time as possible in the backyard, digging in the coal-stained dirt. But I wasn't dancing for pill bugs anymore. I was digging for a foundation. I was trying to find a way to be solid in a town that was built on residuals. 

I thought about the King. I thought about the white jumpsuit and the spotlights. I realized that my stage was going to be different, but the light was going to be the same. I was going to be the one who controlled the story. I wasn't going to be a "Lost Child" or a "Pregnant Teen." I was going to be the architect of a survival that no one in Somerville could predict.

I remember one night, about two weeks after the gas station bathroom, I went out to Bill’s car. The blue Impala was parked in its usual spot at the gas station, the white vinyl top glowing in the fluorescent light of the pumps. I sat in the passenger seat, the heater roaring, the dry heat making my skin feel tight. Bill was talking about something—a part for a truck, a girl at the school, a plan for the weekend. His voice was a flat, comforting drone, the sound of a boy who hadn't yet realized that the air had solidified.

I looked at him, really looked at him, and I saw the simplicity of his trajectory. He would work at the gas station, he would buy a newer car, he would marry a girl who didn't ask too many questions, and he would eventually inhale enough of the RCA plant exhaust that he would disappear into the grey-black soil of Indiana. He was a simple element, stable and predictable.

"You okay, Lisa?" he asked, his hand reaching out to touch my hair. "You look... different tonight."

"I'm fine, Bill," I said, my voice as cool and as hard as the limestone in the quarry. "I'm just planning."

"Planning what?" he laughed, taking a drag of his Camel, the orange glow illuminating the line of his jaw.

"A performance," I said.

He didn't ask what I meant. He didn't have the dictionary for it. He just turned up the radio, a Bad Company song about being a "Ready for Love," and I leaned back against the white vinyl, watching the shadows of the telephone poles flicker across the glass. I was fourteen years old, I was pregnant, and I was already the Madam of my own internal empire. I was the one holding the ledger, and the debt I was recording was one that no boy in a blue Impala could ever hope to pay.

As we drove away from the gas station, the town of Somerville falling behind us into the dark, I felt the first real shift in my own biology—not a movement of the life inside me, but a movement of the resolve. I wasn't afraid of the collaps anymore. I was the one who was going to decide when the ground was firm. I looked at my reflection in the side mirror, the distorted, silvered image of a girl who had already survived the worst the world could throw at her. I wasn't invisible. I was essential. I was solid.

And for the first time in my life, I wasn't waiting for the storm to pass. I was the storm. 

I gripped the door handle of the Impala, the cold steel a reminder of the boundaries I was building. I didn't need a savior, and I didn't need a sanctuary. I needed a gravity that was entirely my own. And as the car cut through the Indiana fog, carrying its two passengers into the night, I knew that the "Invisible Girl" had finally found her stage. It was a stage made of secrets and scars and steely determination, and I was going to be the greatest performer Somerville had ever seen. 

I closed my eyes and whispered the loneliest promise one more time.

*You're on your own. And so am I.*

The car hit an expansion joint on the highway, a sharp *thump-thump* that vibrated through the chassis. I didn't flinch. I just held on to the door and waited for the next stop.
