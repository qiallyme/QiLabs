
# Chapter 7 — The Musician


Technically, I wasn't allowed to be in the bar. But in Indiana in the late seventies, "allowed" was a flexible, porous concept, a thin membrane of legality that was easily breached if you knew which specific side door to slip through and possessed the particular kind of hollow-boned confidence that comes from growing up in a house where your primary requirement was invisibility. In a place like *The Hayloft*, if you held your head high enough, kept your back straight, and didn't flinch when the neon beer signs buzzed, people simply assumed you belonged. They saw the posture and ignored the birth certificate. I was an expert at the camouflage of belonging.

The place sat on the ragged edge of town, a cavernous, windowless hall made of rain-stained cedar and corrugated metal. It smelled of damp sawdust, stale Michelob that had seen too many floorboards, and a thick, high-tar cigarette smoke that hung in the air like a permanent low-lying fog, catching the light of the jukebox in sickly green and yellow rays. It was a temple of blue-collar release, a pressurized container where the men from the RCA plant and the coal mines came to exhume the stress of their shifts, and where women came to be reminded that they still had a pulse beyond the kitchen sink. The floor was permanently tacky, a layer of spilled Miller High Life and industrial grit that held onto the soles of your shoes with a desperate, sticky intensity. The speakers overhead didn't just play music; they rattled with a bass line so heavy and prehistoric that it felt like it was trying to correct the rhythm of my own heart, overriding my nervous system with a louder, more certain pulse.

I stood in the deep, velvet-black shadows near the stage, the darkness a familiar, comforting sanctuary. I was fifteen years old, wearing a pair of borrowed Levi’s that were too tight across the hips and a coat of Maybelline mascara that was already beginning to smudge and run in the thick Indiana humidity. I should have been at home, tucked away in the safety of my room, listening to the rhythmic sawing of my father’s snoring or navigating the aftermath of one of my mother’s "fits"—those episodic breakages where she’d scream at the peeling wallpaper or rearrange the plastic furniture in the yard under the moonlight. But the bar was better. The bar was loud, yes, but it was a predictable, choreographed kind of noise. Unlike the trailer, where the explosion was always a surprise, the bar had a script. It had a beginning, a middle, and a set of rules that everyone followed.

And I was there to watch the sun.

His name was Jack. He was twenty-one, the lead singer and centerpiece of *Southern Comfort*, a local band that churned out covers of Lynyrd Skynyrd, Bob Seger, and the Allman Brothers with a raw, desperate energy that felt like a localized riot. He stood center stage, bathed in a harsh, clinical red gel light that made his skin look like it was slowly oxidizing, turning him into a creature of fire and shadow. He wasn't classically handsome; his face was a collection of sharp, predatory angles—a high, narrow forehead, a nose that looked like it had been broken and reset by a man with no medical training, and eyes that were constantly scanning the room for a reaction, a target, or an opening.

But he had a solid black Gibson guitar slung low over his hips, the strap a thick, worn leather that cut across his Western shirt. When he played, he didn't just perform; he occupied the space, pushing the air out of the room with the force of his presence. He didn't look at his hands as they moved across the fretboard with a liquid, effortless precision; he looked at the crowd with a fierce, possessive intensity. He had a way of leaning into the vintage chrome microphone that made it seem like he was sharing an intimate, dangerous secret with every woman in the room. He was the gravity I had seen in Elvis at the Coliseum, scaled down and made visceral in the smoke-haze of a dive bar.

What captivated me most, however, was his hair. It was thick, wavy, and dark, but it was streaked with premature gray at the temples that caught the red stage lights like veins of silver in a coal mine. To my naive, fifteen-year-old eyes, that gray wasn't a genetic quirk or a sign of stress; it was a badge of office. It meant weight. It meant experience. It meant he had already seen the things I was only starting to guess at, that he had already survived the collisions I was still trying to outrun. I believed, with the desperate intensity of a girl with no reference for safety, that gray hair was a sign of wisdom—it was a promise that he was a man who knew how to navigate the world without breaking everything he touched. I saw the gray and ignored the sharp angles.

I watched him sing, his voice raspy and textured like industrial sandpaper, a sound that felt like it was scraping the top layer of my skin off. Every time he hit a high note, his neck would strain, the tendons standing out like cold-drawn wire. I felt the familiar, magnetic pull of the gravity. I didn't see a twenty-one-year-old boy with a guitar and a mediocre band; I saw an exit strategy. I saw a vehicle that could carry me past the limits of Somerville and the shadow of the RCA plant and into a life that had high-gloss edges and permanent spotlights. 

After the final set, the stage lights died down to a dull, humming amber glow. The house music took over, some disco track that felt flimsy and pathetic after the raw power of the band. Jack came off the platform, his Western shirt damp with sweat, the smell of Marlboros and cheap whiskey following him like a wake. He wiped his face with a grey, lint-speckled towel and looked around the room, his gaze moving past the tables of drunk factory workers and the women who were leaning forward with a desperate hope. Then, his eyes landed on me, standing in the shadows by the speaker stack.

He didn't look through me like I was a piece of the architecture. He didn't look past me like I was a residual. He stopped, leaned his guitar against a wooden pillar, and smiled—a slow, crooked, predatory expression that felt like a match being struck in a perfectly dark room. It was the first time in fifteen years that a man had looked at me and seen something other than a target or a ghost.

"You're the girl who knows every single word of *Simple Man*," he said. His voice was lower than it had been on the stage, vibrating with a confidence I hadn't yet learned to distrust. It was a voice that promised a world where I was the main character.

"I pay attention," I said. My voice was thin, a high-pitched reed in the noise, but I made sure it didn't tremble. I used the "mirror face" I’d practiced in my bedroom, reflecting his own confidence back at him.

"I can see that," he said, taking a step toward me, closing the distance until I could smell the leather of his guitar strap and the copper tang of the mic. He reached out and touched my arm. His fingers were rough, calloused into leather from the steel strings of the Gibson, and their heat seemed to sink through my skin and settle directly into my marrow. "You don't belong back here in the shadows, darlin'. A girl with eyes like yours ought to be sitting where the light can find her. A girl with eyes that sharp shouldn't be invisible. Come sit with me."

In that moment, the ambient, grinding noise of *The Hayloft*—the clinking of Miller bottles, the raucous, desperate laughter of men who hated their lives, the mechanical hum of the neon signs—simply vanished. All I could feel was the physical weight of his hand on my skin and the terrifying, beautiful, intoxicating sensation of being *chosen*. The man with the black guitar, the man who controlled the physics of the room, was reaching into my invisibility and pulling me out. He was offering me a shape.

I didn't see the red flags that were practically waving in the humid air. I didn't wonder why a twenty-one-year-old man was spending his break with a fifteen-year-old girl in the corner of a dive bar. I didn't see the cold, clinical calculation in his narrow eyes or the hardness in the set of his jaw that mirrored my father’s. I didn't recognize that the "light" he was offering was just another form of ownership.

I just walked out of the shadows and into his amber-gel light, grateful to finally possess a physical mass that someone else noticed. I walked toward his table, my knees shaking in the tight Levi’s, feeling like I had finally, truly, begun my life. I didn't realize that the brightest lights cast the longest, most permanent shadows, or that the man who pulls you out of the dark is often the one who knows exactly how to put you back in. I was fifteen years old, and I had just been recruited into a different kind of factory, one where the product was my own silence and the production line never stopped.

I sat at the booth with him, the red vinyl cracked and held together with silver duct tape that scratched at the backs of my legs. Jack ordered a beer and a Coke for me, sliding the glass across the table like a chip in a high-stakes game. He talked about "the road"—about Evansville, about Indianapolis, about a world where the music never stopped and the money was always "upfront." He spoke with a rhythmic, hypnotic cadence that made the trailer in Somerville seem like a fever dream I was finally waking up from.

"You got a look about you, Lisa," he said, leaning over the table, the gray at his temples catching the light again. "A look that says you're ready for more than this town has to offer. I've seen girls like you before. You're a star waiting for a sky."

I drank the Coke, the bubbles sharp and biting against my throat, and I believed him. I believed every word of the script. I believed that my "visibility" was a gift he was giving me, not a resource he was staking a claim to. I looked at the black guitar leaning against the pillar and I felt a surge of ambition that was colder and harder than anything I’d felt in the backyard dirt.

I wasn't just Claude’s daughter or a pregnant statistic. I was the girl who sat with the musician. I was the one who had been pulled out of the shadows. I felt a sense of gravity that was entirely external, a weight that came from his gaze and his attention. It was intoxicating. It was also an addiction I didn't even know I was developing.

That night, when he drove me home in his battered van—a vehicle that smelled of amplifiers and old coffee—I didn't look at the RCA plant as we passed. I didn't look at the coal dust. I looked at Jack, at the way his hands moved on the steering wheel, and I felt a sense of peace that was entirely false. I thought I had found the exit. I thought I had found the savior who would stand between me and the world.

I didn't know that the savior was just the first version of the warden. I didn't know that the "light" he promised would soon turned into the heavy pink gels of the Busybody, where I would still be sitting where the light could find me, but for a very different price. 

I thanked him at the end of the driveway, my voice finally holding a shred of the confidence I’d been practicing. I walked toward the trailer, the metal steps feeling lighter under my feet. I didn't freeze when I heard the door click open. I didn't shrink into the hallway. I walked into the kitchen, where my mother was sitting in the dark, and I didn't even acknowledge the silence.

"I found a man, Mom," I whispered to the dark.

She didn't answer. She probably didn't even hear me. She was too busy cutting her own ghosts away. But I didn't care. I was the Visible Girl now. I had the red-gel light in my eyes, and I was already planning my debut on a stage that was much bigger than *The Hayloft*. 

The Monster was still a secret, hidden behind the black guitar and the crooked smile. But the hook was set, and I was swimming toward the bait with everything I had. I was fifteen years old, and I was already an expert in the business of pleasure. I just didn't know yet who was going to be the one paying the price.
