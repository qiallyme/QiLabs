
# Chapter 2 — Claude


My father, Claude, lived at the mercy of his own internal chemistry. He was a man-sized pendulum, swinging wildly between a manic, sentimental generosity and a cold, creative destructive rage. When the pendulum was on the upswing, he was the sun—bright, blinding, and warm. In those seasons, we were the planets orbiting him, our faces turned toward his light, grateful for the temporary thaw in the atmosphere. We lived in the brief, beautiful illusion of a normal life. The air in the trailer would lose its pressurized edge, replaced by the smell of baking bread or the sound of the radio playing something other than the news. The tension in my mother’s shoulders would drop an inch, and I could play with my dolls on the living room rug without feeling like I was an obstacle in his path.

The highlight of that season came on an unseasonably warm Saturday in 1974. I was seven years old, a skinny girl with knees that were always skinned and eyes that were already too good at reading a room. The Indiana sky was a vast, unbroken blue, the kind of clear that made everything on the ground look sharper, more defined.

"Get dressed, Lisa," he told me that morning. He was sitting at the edge of his bed, leaning forward, his elbows resting on his knees. He was wearing his clean work shirt, the one without the grease stains on the cuffs. His eyes were bright with the kind of secret that made him look younger, less tired. The usual heavy bags under his eyes seemed to have retreated, and his voice lacked the gravelly, impatient edge it usually carried. "Wash your face. We're going to see the King."

I didn't ask who he meant. In 1974, in a house like ours, there was only one King.

I wore my best Sunday dress—an excruciatingly scratchy polyester thing with a stiff, lace collar that felt like a saw against my neck. Every time I turned my head, the lace would bite into the tender skin beneath my jaw. The fabric was a pale, sickly yellow that looked washed out against my winter-pale skin. My patent leather shoes were half a size too small, pinching my toes until they throbbed with a dull, rhythmic ache. I could feel the stiff leather of the heels digging into my skin with every step, but I didn't say a word. I sat on the edge of the couch, my hands folded in my lap, waiting for him to finish his coffee. In our house, you didn't complain about the price of a gift, especially a gift that felt as fragile and as rare as my father's good mood.

We climbed into his truck, just the two of us. The cab smelled of old coffee that had been spilled and forgotten, high-tar tobacco, and the metallic dust he brought home from the RCA plant—a scent that had become the permanent wallpaper of my life. The upholstery was a cracked, sun-faded vinyl that stuck to the backs of my legs. The dashboard was a landscape of loose change, tattered receipts, and a small, magnetic Jesus whose paint was peeling from the heat. As we drove toward Evansville, the radio played *Kentucky Rain*, and Claude sang along with a desperate, heavy-hearted gusto. He tapped his greased-stained fingers on the steering wheel, his wedding ring clicking rhythmically against the plastic. He wasn't drinking that morning; he was high on the sheer anticipation of the spectacle, his foot heavy on the gas pedal as if he were trying to outrun the gravity of Somerville.

The Coliseum was a massive, grey concrete bowl that seemed to swallow the late afternoon sky. It was a brutalist structure, all hard angles and rain-stained cement, surrounded by a sea of asphalt that shimmered with heat haze. Thousands of people were streaming toward the entrances, a slow-moving river of polyester, denim, and excitement. As we walked toward the gate, I held his hand—his palm was a map of rough callouses and deep-set engine grease that no amount of Lava soap could ever truly remove. His grip was tight, possessive. He walked with a swagger that day, the swagger of a man who had procured tickets to the greatest show on earth, a man who for once wasn't just another number on a factory line, but a father who could provide the impossible.

Inside, the air was a solid, vibrating thing. It didn't smell like the usual Somerville cocktail of coal dust and woodsmoke. It smelled of expensive perfume, spilled beer, popcorn butter, and a raw, electric hysteria that made the hair on my arms stand up. Our seats were high up in the rafters, looking down into the deep, dark well of the stage. The lights went down, a collective gasp swept through the thousands of people, and the opening strains of *Also sprach Zarathustra* began to rumble through the floorboards. The bass was so loud it felt like it was reorganizing the atoms in my body, a low-frequency roar that I felt in my teeth and in the marrow of my bones.

The screaming didn't start like a sound; it broke like a physical wall of noise that hit you in the chest and stole your breath. It was a high-pitched, desperate sound, a sound of unadulterated hunger.

Then, he appeared.

Elvis Presley stepped into the white-hot spotlights, a creature made of rhinestones and sound. The white jumpsuit was blinding, catching every beam of light in the arena and shattering it into a million pieces. He didn't just walk; he glided across the stage with a liquid, dangerous grace. He was larger than any human I had ever seen, a god who had descended into the concrete bowl of our lives. Even from the top of the Coliseum, I could see the flashbulbs popping like a thousand tiny suns, illuminating him in jagged, staccato bursts.

I sat on the very edge of my seat, my heart hammering against my ribs with a frantic, mechanical rhythm. I watched him wipe the sweat from his forehead with a silk scarf and toss it casually into the front row. I watched grown women—women who looked like my mother, women who looked like grandmothers who spent their Sundays baking pies—dive for that piece of fabric. They were clawing and weeping, their faces distorted by a need I didn't yet understand, just to touch something he had touched. It was a religious ecstasy, a communal madness that seemed to lift the roof off the building.

I looked up at my father. He was staring at the stage with an expression I had never seen before. It wasn't envy, not exactly. It was a fierce, desperate recognition. He wasn't singing anymore. He was absolutely still, his mouth slightly open, his knuckles white as he gripped the railing in front of him. He saw something in the man in the white jumpsuit that he wanted for himself—the ability to command a room, to hold ten thousand people in the palm of his hand, without ever having to raise a voice or a fist. He wanted the worship. He wanted the absolute focus. He wanted to be the one whose every gesture was a law.

"Look at him, Lisa," Claude shouted over the roar of the music, his voice cracking with an emotion I couldn't name. "That's a man. That’s what a man looks like when he can have anything he wants."

But I was looking at the physics of the room, the way the light followed him, the way the sound seemed to emanate from his very skin.

I realized then that Elvis didn't have to scream to be heard. He didn't have to throw plates or break furniture to get attention. He didn't have to create a "climate of fear" to ensure compliance. He just stood there, raised a finger or curled his lip in that famous, half-sneer, and ten thousand people held their breath in perfect, terrifying unison. He had a gravity that pulled everything toward him. He was the center of the universe, and everything else in the building—the people, the lights, the air—was just orbiting his light. It was a power that looked like a choice, not a threat.

The concert ended in a blur of gold and white and the thunder of applause that felt like a physical weight. We filed out into the cool night air, the silence of the parking lot feeling heavy and unnatural after the roar of the arena. Thousands of cars were trying to leave at once, their headlights cutting through the exhaust fumes like searchlights. 

On the drive home, the adrenaline began to curdle in the cab of the truck. The silence returned, but it wasn't a peaceful silence. It was the silence of the decay. The light in my father's eyes had begun to dim as the high of the spectacle wore off, leaving him irritable and drained. His movements became sharp and impatient again. Every time he shifted gears, the truck lurched, and his jaw would tighten. The neon of the city faded into the dark, flat stretches of the Indiana cornfields, the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of the tires on the expansion joints of the highway marking the countdown to our return. 

The cold reality of Somerville was waiting for us with its coal dust and its windowless factory. I could see the glow of the RCA plant on the horizon, a sickly orange haze that signaled the end of our license to pretend.

I looked out the window at the passing telephone poles, rhythmic and darker than the night sky. The scratches from my polyester dress had turned into a burning itch, and my feet were throbbing in the too-small shoes. I realized then that my father’s power was cheap. It was loud, messy, and dependent on a very specific kind of fear. He could make us move, he could make us quiet, but he couldn't make us lean in. He couldn't create the kind of gravity that made people claw for a piece of his scarf. His power was a weapon; Elvis’s power was a destination.

I closed my eyes and replayed the concert in my head—the white jumpsuit, the flashbulbs, the way the air felt like it was on fire. I didn't want to be the woman fighting for a scarf, a secondary character in someone else's drama. I didn't want to be the child shrinking in the passenger seat of a truck that smelled of tobacco and low expectations. 

I looked at my father’s hands on the wheel, the grease under his nails a permanent record of the factory floor, and I felt a sudden, cold clarity. I didn't just want to escape Somerville. I wanted the gravity. I wanted to be the one who made the room go still when I walked onto the stage. I wanted to be the one who Shattered the light and held it in my hand. I didn't know then how much it would cost or how many monsters I would have to marry to get there, but as the truck pulled into the gravel driveway and the familiar shadow of the trailer loomed over us, I knew that the "invisible girl" was already planning her debut.

I unbuckled my seatbelt, the metallic *clack* sounding like a gavel. 

"Did you like it, Lisa?" he asked, his voice tired now, the sun having set on his season of sun.

"I liked it, Dad," I said, my voice as small as the girl in the yellow dress. 

But I wasn't thinking about the songs. I was thinking about the light. I was thinking about the way ten thousand people had looked at one man and seen a god. And I realized that if I could learn the technicality of that light—the way to hold a room, the way to stand so that the shadows didn't dare to touch you—I would never have to be invisible again. I would never have to wait for the pendulum to swing. I would be the pendulum.

I opened the door and stepped out onto the gravel. The air smelled of coal smoke and wet soybeans. I walked toward the trailer, the small shoes pinching my toes, but I walked with my head up, my eyes fixed on the distant, flickering lights of the plant. I wasn't just Claude’s daughter anymore. I was a student of the King, and my education was only just beginning.

I spent the next week in the backyard, digging in the coal-stained dirt. But I wasn't building fortresses of twigs anymore. I was building a stage. I would stand on a flat stone, the Indiana heat baking the top of my head, and I would perform for the pill bugs and the telephone poles. I would curl my lip and raise a finger, imagining the roar of a crowd that hadn't yet been born. I would sing the songs I’d heard in the truck, my voice a thin, high-pitched reed against the thundering of the passing trains.

My mother would watch me from the kitchen window, her hand holding a dishcloth, her face a mask of weary confusion. She didn't understand the physics of my play. She didn't see the rhinestones I was imagining or the white-hot spotlights I was constructing out of the afternoon sun. She only saw a skinny girl in a dirty dress, dancing in a patch of dirt that would never grow anything.

But I could feel the gravity starting to take root. I could feel the center of my own universe beginning to solidify.

I realized that the "Invisible Girl" wasn't a choice; it was a disguise. It was a camouflage I could use to navigate the minefields of my father’s temper and the industrial grey of Somerville. I would wear the yellow dress, I would keep the small shoes, and I would wait for the lights to come up. Because one day, I wouldn't be looking up at the King in a concrete bowl. I would be the one standing in the white-hot center, and the only residuals I would care about were the ones I produced myself.

The walk back into the house felt different that Saturday night. The brown paneling of the living room seemed smaller, the yellow light from the kitchen lamp more dim. The transition from the scale of the Coliseum back to the scale of the trailer was a physical weight, a slow-motion collapse of possibility. I watched my mother set the table, her movements returning to that jerky, wired rhythm I’d seen a thousand times. The "license to pretend" was expiring, minute by minute, as the night took over.

Claude sat in his recliner, the one with the worn armrests and the smell of stale smoke. He didn't turn on the television. He just sat there in the dark for a long time, his eyes fixed on the far wall. I wondered if he was still replaying the music, or if he was already feeling the weight of the Monday morning factory whistle. I wondered if he realized that he had shown me a map out of the very life he was trying to protect.

I went to the bathroom and peeled off the scratchy polyester dress. It left red, irritated welts around my neck and across my shoulder blades—a physical receipt of the day's beauty. I washed the coal dust from my knees, the grey water swirling down the drain in a slow, hypnotic spiral. I looked at myself in the medicine cabinet mirror, the glass speckled with water spots and aged along the edges. I didn't see a seven-year-old girl. I saw a vessel for a future I hadn't yet named.

That night, I dreamt of rhinestones and limestone. I dreamt of a world where the ground was solid and the lights were permanent. I dreamt of a stage that was taller than the RCA plant and a voice that could drown out the sound of the coal trains.

In the dream, my father wasn't holding my hand. He was just another face in the crowd, looking up at me with that same fierce, desperate recognition. And I wasn't a girl in a yellow dress. I was the gravity. I was the center. I was the one who controlled the swing of the pendulum.

When I woke up on Sunday morning, the Indiana humidity was back, and the smell of the plant was heavy in the air. The season of sun was over. My father was in the kitchen, his voice already carrying that familiar, gravelly edge as he complained about the price of eggs. My mother was silent, her back to him, her shoulders hunched. The trailer was back to its normal atmosphere, a space governed by the physics of fear and the residuals of a life half-lived.

But as I sat at the table and ate my breakfast, I felt a secret warmth in my chest. I looked at my hands, clean now, but I could still feel the phantom weight of the King's scarf. I knew something they didn't know. I had seen the white-hot center of the universe, and I knew that the "Invisible Girl" was just a role I was playing until the stage was ready.

I finished my milk and stood up, my back straight, my head up. I walked past my father, careful not to touch his chair, and went out into the backyard. I didn't go to the twigs or the fortresses. I went to the flat stone.

I stood there, the sun beating down on the charcoal-black dirt, and I waited for the first train to pass. When it came, the ground shaking beneath my feet, the roar of the engines filling the valley, I didn't freeze. I didn't hide. I stood my ground, my eyes fixed on the horizon, and I began to dance. I danced in the coal dust, I danced in the shadow of the plant, I danced for a crowd of telephone poles and pill bugs. I was the King of the backyard, the Madam of the dirt, the architect of a future that Somerville couldn't reach.

And for the first time in my life, I wasn't afraid of the collapse. I was the one who was going to break the ground.
