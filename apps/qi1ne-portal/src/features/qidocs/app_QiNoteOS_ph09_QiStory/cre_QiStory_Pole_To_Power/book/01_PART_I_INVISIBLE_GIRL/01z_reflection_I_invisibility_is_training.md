
# Reflection I — What I Didn’t Know Then
## Invisibility Is Training


When you grow up in a house where the weather is determined exclusively by someone else’s erratic, chemically-fueled chemistry, you don’t just learn to cope. You undergo a fundamental, structural rewiring that alters the very circuitry of your nervous system. You become more than a child; you become a meteorologist of the human spirit, a specialized technician of the atmosphere. You learn to read the microscopic, subterranean shifts in atmospheric pressure—the way the afternoon light hits a half-empty bottle of vodka on the Formica table, the specific, heavy thud of a steel-toed boot on a metal step that signals the night’s potential for violence, the precise, low-frequency hum of a silence that precedes a storm. You learn to interpret the vibration of the floorboards as a direct, non-verbal message from the universe: *Safe* or *Disappear*.

I didn't know then that I was in training. I didn't realize that every hour spent huddled in the shadow of the hallway was a masterclass in the technicality of survival.

At the time, I thought I was just hiding, a shameful retreat into the margins. I thought the act of flattening my spine against the hollow-core doorframe, making myself as thin and as two-dimensional as possible, was a sign of my own smallness—a biological failure of courage. I believed that because I couldn't stop the plates from hitting the wall, because I couldn't mend the jagged, raw, bleeding edges of my mother’s mental collapse, I was simply a casualty of the geography. I felt like a residual, a piece of industrial fallout from my father’s life. But looking back from the vantage point of the Madam’s chair, I see that I wasn't a casualty; I was an adaptation. I was an evolutionary response to an environment built on coal dust and erratic rage. I was becoming a master architect of the void, learning the physics of taking up space without ever actually being seen.

The "Invisible Child" is not a role one chooses out of preference or natural inclination. It is a tactical necessity in a world where the adults have converted their own needs and failures into weapons of mass destruction. In a healthy home, a child’s need is a biological call for connection, a signal that triggers a response of protection and nurture. In a house like ours, localized in the oppressive, metallic shadow of the RCA plant and the hollowed-out landscape of the coal mines, a child’s need is a direct threat to a parent’s fragile, precarious stability. 

If asking for a pair of shoes that fit triggers an alcoholic blackout because the money has already been spent on beer, or if needing a hot meal causes a mother to reach for the sewing scissors because she can no longer bear the weight of her own life, the child’s brain performs a brutal, irreversible mathematical calculation: *Need equals Threat.* To be wanting is to be dangerous. To be hungry is to be an obstacle. Therefore, to stay alive, one must systematically eliminate the need. You learn to suppress your own hunger until the nerve endings in your stomach stop growling and simply go numb. You stop crying—you excise the very impulse to weep—because the noise of your grief only attracts the attention of the monster. 

I became a human thermostat for people who had lost their own ability to regulate the temperature. If the room was getting too hot, the tension rising toward a physical explosion, I cooled it with the ice of my own calculated silence. If the atmosphere was too cold, a freezing draft of neglect that threatened to turn into a collapse, I warmed it with the frantic, performative compliance of the "good girl," the one who cleaned the kitchen until her knuckles bled and whose grades were a desperate plea for a temporary safety.

This is more than a survival strategy; it is an economy. It is the trade of identity for the privilege of physical safety, a transaction conducted in the dark, without witnesses. 

Somerville was a town that flourished on this kind of silence. The community didn't want to look at the trailer parks or the broken, vibration-scarred men walking out of the coal mines; it wanted the gears of the RCA factory to keep turning with a quiet efficiency and for the main street to look regular. An Invisible Child is a massive asset to a town like that. We are the girls who don't call the police when the screaming starts, because we know the police are just men with their own barometers. We are the girls who don't tell the teacher why we have blue-and-yellow bruises on our wrists, inventing elaborate stories about falling off the porch or walking into a door. We are the girls who treat an eighteen-year-old boy’s attention in a darkened Chevy Impala as a form of salvation rather than a boundary violation. We were the grease that kept the engine of the status quo running without noise.

What I didn't know then was that by silencing my own voice to keep the peace, I was slowly but systematically erasing my own marrow. Every time I reflected a man’s desire back to him—whether it was my father’s need for a "funny" and compliant audience or Bill’s need for a "special," deeper girl to exhibit in his passenger seat—I was practicing what I now call the "Mirror Effect." I was learning how to be a hollow vessel, a polished surface of reflection that gave them everything they needed to feel powerful and real, while I kept the actual person named Lisa locked in a dark, interior room where no one could ever touch her.

The stage in the Busybody was only a natural extension of the linoleum kitchen floor in Somerville. The lights, the music, the clear plastic heels—those were just more sophisticated tools for the same fundamental job. Men are drawn to mirrors. They mistake the silence of a girl for mystery. They mistake her compliance for devotion. They believe that they are the sun and she is the moon, reflecting their light, and they never once ask what lies on the dark side of that moon.

But reflection is not connection. It is the muscle memory of the hunted.

I walked out of my childhood with a doctorate in disappearance. I knew how to make a man feel like the center of the universe while feeling absolutely nothing myself. I knew how to navigate the most volatile, mercury-rising atmospheres without ever triggering the explosion. I thought these skills were my power. I thought they were the "gravity" I had seen in Elvis—the ability to hold a room, to command attention, to make the world go still. I thought that by controlling the reflection, I was controlling the man.

I didn't realize that real gravity is not just about the ability to pull things toward you; it is about the mass you possess yourself. When you spend fifteen years making yourself into a ghost, you lose your internal mass. You become a shell. You find that when you finally want to scream—truly scream for your own life—you have forgotten the vocabulary. You have traded your voice for the privilege of not being hit, and you find the silence is much harder to break than any bone.

Invisibility protected me from the monsters in my hallway. It kept me alive when the ground was hollow and the mines were collapsing. But it prepared me with terrifying, mechanical precision for the stage, where my invisibility would be commodified, sold by the hour in twenty-dollar increments, and eventually used to hollow out what little of the marrow I had left. This wasn't a choice; it was a trajectory. I was moving from one "invisible" room to another, mistake-proofed by my own training, until the day I realized that the mirror was starting to crack and the person behind it was starting to wake up.

I spent my youth learning to take up no space at all. I spent the rest of my life learning how to survive the explosion when I finally decided to grow. Somerville taught me how to disappear; the Pole taught me how to be seen. But the price of that transition was a debt I am still paying off, one word at a time.

The "Doctorate in Disappearance" also taught me a profound lesson about the nature of those who hunt. When you are invisible, you see things that the "visible" world misses. You see the gaps between what people say and what they do. You see the way Claude would talk about the "smell of money" while his hands shook with the smell of failure. You see the way Bill would talk about me being "special" while he looked at my body as a piece of equipment to be used. You see the town of Somerville as a collection of masks, each one more fragile than the last.

Invisibility is a vantage point. It allows you to study the monsters while they are busy ignoring you. I learned to categorize them long before I knew the psychological terminology. I knew the difference between a "Drunk with a Grin" and a "Drunk with a Knife." I knew how to tell when a man’s voice was dropping into that low-octave growl that signaled a landslide. I was a researcher in the laboratory of human cruelty, and my own skin was the litmus paper.

What I didn't know then was that this expertise would eventually become my most valuable asset in the escrow business. The "Screening" process I developed as a Madam was just a formalized version of the "Reading the Air" I did as a child. When a man called the service, I wasn't listening to his request; I was listening to the pressure in his voice. I was looking for the microscopic shift in tone that signaled a predator. I was using the scars from Somerville to protect the girls from the same fate I had endured.

But being a Madam is just another way of being invisible. You are the "Invisible Hand" that moves the pieces on the board. You are still the one holding the ledger in the dark. You are still the one whose safety is dependent on someone else’s compliance. I had traded the kitchen floor for the dispatch office, but the physics were the same. I was still managing the atmosphere instead of living in it.

Real power, the kind I finally found on the night of the knife scene, is the ability to stop being a mirror. It’s the ability to break the glass and let the light hit you directly, regardless of the consequences. It’s the moment you realize that taking up space is not a tactical error; it’s a biological right. 

Somerville and the RCA plant tried to teach me that I was a residual, a byproduct of the coal smoke and the industrial grey. They tried to teach me that my value was dependent on my ability to reflect the sun. But in the dark of that Indiana trailer, I was secretly building my own furnace. I was learning that invisibility is a great training ground, but a terrible home. You can only stay in the shadows for so long before you forget the color of your own skin.

I left Somerville with a bag full of secrets and a heart made of cold, calculated survival. I thought I was free because I was leaving the trailer. I didn't realize that I was carrying the trailer inside me, the brown paneling and the smell of grease forming a permanent internal geography. I was free to move, but I wasn't free to be. I was still a passenger in a blue Impala, even when I was the one driving.

The "Invisible Girl" would have to be systematically dismantled, piece by jagged piece, until only the woman remained. And that woman—the one who could look a monster in the eye and say "Get Out"—would be the only King I would ever need to worship. The View from the Pole is about seeeing, yes. But it's also about being seen. It's about the moment the spotlight hits you and you don't flinch. It's about the realization that you have finally, truly, paid the toll in full.
