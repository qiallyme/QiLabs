
# Chapter 3 — Mother


My mother was a ghost long before she finally abandoned the physical world. She moved through the trailer like a draft, touching the surfaces of our life without ever really holding onto them. She was a master of the invisible labor—the one who kept the peace, who absorbed the static of my father’s moods, who made herself smaller and smaller until she was practically translucent. She lived in the margins so that my father could occupy the center, an architecture of sacrifice that I watched with a mixture of pity and a cold, growing terror. She was a thermostat that only cooled, never warmed, her presence a constant attempt to lower the atmospheric pressure before it reached the point of explosion. She moved with a soft, apologetic shuffle, as if the very act of displacing air were a personal failing.

But there is a limit to how much you can compress a human soul before the structure finally fails. There is a point where the weight of the silence becomes greater than the strength of the vessel.

Her breakage wasn’t a slow, manageable leak. It wasn't a series of small, weeping cracks that we could ignore or plaster over. It was a sudden, catastrophic snap.

I came home from school on a Tuesday afternoon in early October. The Indiana autumn was in full swing, the sky a bruised, purple-grey that promised an early winter. The air outside was crisp, smelling of burning leaves, damp river mud, and the distant, metallic sigh of the RCA plant—a heavy, industrial scent that always seemed to linger longer in the cooling air. The gravel in the driveway crunched under my school shoes, a lonely, isolated sound in the afternoon quiet. The house was still when I stepped through the door, the air inside smelling of Murphy’s Oil Soap and the faint, lingering grease of last night’s bacon.

But it was a different kind of silence than usual. It wasn't the held-breath, high-alert silence of waiting for Claude to come home Angry. It didn't have that sharp, electric vibration that precedes a storm. It was a flat, dead silence—the silence of a clock that had finally run out of gears to turn, a mechanical resignation that felt heavy on my skin.

I found her in the small kitchen. The room was bathed in the flickering, judgmental light of the overhead fluorescent tube, a cold, clinical hum that seemed to highlight every dent in the linoleum and every scratch on the Formica countertops. She wasn't standing at the stove, and she wasn't doing the dishes. She was standing perfectly still in front of the narrow, silver-framed mirror that hung over the sink, the one she usually used to check her lipstick before church or to tuck a stray lock of dark hair behind her ear when she thought no one was looking.

She was holding a pair of heavy, professional-grade sewing scissors in her right hand. The metal was dull and pitted, a cold, industrial grey against the unnatural, translucent paleness of her skin. The scissors were designed for cutting through heavy denim and thick wool, their blades long and sharp, their handles uncomfortable and oversized for her small hands.

"Mom?" I asked. My voice felt small and intrusive in the heavy air, a pebble dropped into a deep, stagnant well. I shifted my backpack, the weight of the textbooks—geometry, history, language—digging into my shoulders like a physical manifestation of the expectations I was already carrying. I could feel the sweat cooling on my lower back, the dampness of the Indiana humidity clinging to my hair.

She didn't turn her head. She didn't acknowledge my presence with so much as a blink. Her eyes were fixed on her own reflection, but it didn't look like she was seeing herself. She was looking *through* herself, staring at some point miles behind the silvered glass. She simply raised her arm, her movements slow, deliberate, and jerky, as if she were a puppet being manipulated by a very tired child. She opened the blades of those heavy scissors, slid a thick, dark, wavy lock of her hair between the steel, and clamped down.

The sound was shockingly loud in the small room—a wet, thick mechanical noise that made my stomach turn. It was the sound of something being severed that wasn't meant to be cut. A jagged chunk of dark hair, still soft and healthy-looking, fell to the yellow linoleum floor with a soft, final whisper. It landed near a coffee stain she had missed that morning, looking like a dead bird in the flickering light.

"Mom, stop it. Just... stop." I took a single step forward, the soles of my shoes sticking slightly to the floor. Then I froze. I saw her eyes in the reflection of the mirror. There was no rage in them. There was no grief, no hysteria, no pleading. There was nothing. It was an absolute, terrifying emptiness, a gaze that suggested the person I called "Mother" had already vacated the premises, leaving only this shell to finish the work of demolition. It was the gaze of someone who had decided that the only way to be safe was to be gone.

Another clamp of the blades. Another heavy chunk of hair fell, landing on the toe of her worn blue slipper. The slippers were frayed at the edges, the padding worn thin by years of pacing the small perimeter of the trailer. 

She worked with a frantic, rhythmic efficiency. There was no sobbing, no dramatic screaming, no plea for help. There was only the steady, mechanical sound of the scissors and the soft, growing pile of dark debris at her feet. She hacked at the hair until her scalp began to show through—jagged, angry patches of white skin exposed to the cold, blue light of the kitchen. She looked like a bird that had been partially plucked, a creature caught in the middle of a desperate, ugly transformation.

I stood there in the doorway, my hands gripping the straps of my bag until my knuckles went white and my fingers felt numb. A "normal" child—a child from one of those families I saw in the television specials, where the father didn't throw salt shakers and the mother didn't move like a draft—would have run to her. They would have cried, hugged her legs, and begged her to stop. They would have seen a mother in pain and reached out to hold her.

I didn't move. I felt a cold, hard knot of self-preservation tighten in my chest, a sensation like a stone being swallowed. I wasn't scared for her; I was scared *of* her. I saw a ruin. I saw a building that had been systematically excavated by life, by the RCA plant, and by my father’s oversized appetites until there was nothing left but the dust. I saw her life as a map of what happened when you made silence your only strategy. You eventually disappeared completely, leaving behind a ghost that didn't know how to do anything but continue the work of cutting herself away.

She finally lowered the scissors, her hand dropping to her side with a dull, heavy thud against her thigh. She looked down at the pile of hair on the floor—the dark, soft debris of the woman she had once been, or perhaps the woman she had tried to be for us—and then she turned her head to look at me. Her face was a raw, uneven mask of tragedy, the jagged edges of her remaining hair sticking out at impossible, violent angles. The fluorescent light cast long, skeletal shadows across her features.

"I just wanted to get it off," she whispered. Her voice was thin, dehydrated, a rattle of dry leaves. "It was getting too heavy, Lisa. All of it. The sound, the weight, the waiting. It was just too much to carry. I thought if I cut it off, I might feel lighter."

I nodded slowly, my neck stiff, but I didn't move toward her. I didn't say, "It's okay," because it clearly wasn't. I didn't tell her I loved her, or that she looked beautiful, or that everything would be better in the morning. Those words felt too fragile to survive the pressurized atmosphere of that kitchen. They felt like lies that would only shatter in the silence.

I looked at the dark pile of hair on the linoleum and I made a silent, brutal pact with the girl in the doorway. It was a cold, transactional decision, the kind of logic the Madam would later use to screen a client or negotiate a contract.

*I will not be the wreckage. I will not be the one who cuts herself away to make room for someone else’s rage. I will not be a ghost in my own house.*

I turned and walked away. I didn't say another word. I went to my room and closed the door, the sound of the latch clicking into place feeling like a finality. I left her standing in the center of the mess she had made, though I knew the blueprints for it had been drawn by my father and the town of Somerville long before I was even a thought. I didn't help her sweep it up. I couldn't bring myself to touch it. The hair felt contagious—a lingering, domestic sickness of compliance and erasure that I was determined to outrun.

I sat on my bed and stared at the wood-paneled wall, listening to the rhythmic *scritch-scritch* of her broom as she finally began to clean up the pieces of herself. I realized then that my mother’s strategy of disappearance wasn't a choice; it was a failure of imagination. She hadn't disappeared because she was weak; she had disappeared because she hadn't known how to be seen without being hunted.

I stayed in my room until the factory whistle blew and the sound of the gravel in the driveway signaled my father’s return. I listened to his heavy boots on the steps, the familiar *crunch-crunch-crunch* of his authority. I waited for the explosion when he saw her hair, the roar of his confusion or his disgust.

But the explosion never came. I heard him walk into the kitchen. I heard the refrigerator door open and close. I heard the sound of a beer can being cracked open—that sharp, metallic *hiss* that signaled the beginning of the evening.

"Dinner ready, Martha?" he called out, his voice loud and indifferent.

"In a minute, Claude," she answered.

Her voice sounded the same. The house sounded the same. The RCA plant kept breathing its grey smoke into the Indiana night. She had cut off the weight, she had exposed her scalp to the cold fluorescent light, and he hadn't even noticed. 

That was the final lesson. If you make yourself invisible for long enough, no one will notice when you’re finally gone. You could hack yourself into pieces on the kitchen floor and the man in the next room would still just ask what was for dinner.

I looked at the little fortress of twigs I’d brought in from the backyard, sitting on my nightstand. It looked small and pathetic now, a child’s attempt at protection. I picked it up and threw it into the trash can. I didn't need a fortress of sticks. I needed a gravity that no one could ignore. I needed to be the one who held the scissors, not the one who felt the blade.

I spent the rest of the night practicing my "mirror face"—the expression of absolute, cool detachment I would use to navigate the rest of my childhood. I watched myself in the small mirror on the back of my door, tucking my hair behind my ears, checking the angles of my jaw. I was building a skin that nothing could get through, a surface that would reflect everything and absorb nothing. 

I was ten years old, and I was already preparing for a life where the only person I would ever allow to cut me was myself. 

My mother’s breakage wasn't a tragedy to me; it was a field manual. It was a list of everything I would never allow a man to do to me. It was the blueprint for the "Doctorate in Disappearance" I would eventually use to escape Claude, to survive the Monster, and to build an empire of pleasure where I was the only one who dictated the price of the light.

I didn't sleep much that night. The silence after the broom stopped was worse than the sound of the scissors. It was the silence of a void, a space where a person used to be. In the morning, when I walked into the kitchen for breakfast, she was already there, wearing a headscarf she’d tied in a tight, efficient knot. She didn't look at me. She just pushed a bowl of cereal across the table, her hands steady, her eyes fixed on the coffee pot.

"I’m sorry you had to see that, Lisa," she said, her voice finally holding a shred of the familiar.

"I didn't see anything, Mom," I said.

And I meant it. I had already trained my eyes to look past the wreckage and find the exit. I had already begun to erase her from my map of the world, replacing her with the version of myself that would never, ever be caught in front of a mirror with a pair of sewing scissors. 

I finished my cereal, picked up my bag, and walked out of the house. I didn't look back at the trailer. I didn't look at the RCA plant. I just walked toward the school bus stop, the cold Indiana wind biting at my face, my heart a hard, cold knot of ambition. I wasn't just a girl from Somerville anymore. I was a survivor of the silence, and I was just getting started. 

As the bus pulled away, I watched the reflection of the trailer in the window. It looked smaller and more precarious than ever, a tiny, aging box sitting on a hollow ground. I knew it was only a matter of time before the earth reclaimed it, but by then, I was determined to be somewhere else, standing on a ground that I had built myself, under lights that I had earned the right to control.

The "Invisible Girl" was gone. The "Student of Gravity" was in her place. 

I looked at the girl sitting next to me on the bus—a girl with long, healthy hair and a father who worked at the bank. She was talking about her new doll. She was taking up space. She was loud. I didn't envy her. I pitied her. She didn't know that the ground could collapse. She didn't know that the air could solidify. She didn't know that safety was a temporary license. 

I knew. And that knowledge was the only weapon I had. I gripped the straps of my bag, the weight of the textbooks feeling lighter now, and I smiled. It wasn't a happy smile. It was a predatory smile, the smile of a Madam who had already seen the bottom of the room and knew how to climb back up.

The world was a system of transactions, and I had just made my first one. I had traded my mother’s ghost for my own future. And at ten years old, I knew it was the best deal I would ever make.
